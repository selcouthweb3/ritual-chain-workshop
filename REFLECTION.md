# Reflection: Public, Hidden, and AI vs. Human in a Bounty System

The bounty rubric, reward amount, deadlines, and winner announcement should all be fully public — participants cannot compete fairly without knowing what they are competing for, and transparency in outcomes is what makes the system trustworthy. What should stay hidden until the reveal phase is the content of each participant's answer: premature disclosure lets later entrants copy or improve on earlier submissions, destroying the value of a competitive format. Hiding answers until the commit-reveal phase closes puts all participants on equal footing regardless of submission order.

AI is well-suited to the evaluation step — reading every answer, scoring each against the rubric, and producing a ranked recommendation — because this is exactly the kind of comparative, high-volume judgment work where human attention is error-prone and inconsistent at scale. The AI should surface its reasoning so the owner can inspect it, but the ranked list is advisory, not binding. The financial trigger — calling `finalizeWinner` and releasing funds — must remain a human action, because delegating irreversible money movement to an automated system without human review conflates a tool's recommendation with a decision, and shifts accountability away from anyone who can be held responsible. Keeping humans as the final authorizing step on payouts preserves accountability without sacrificing the efficiency gains that AI evaluation provides.
